The opportunity, and the one rule that shapes it

The SBIR II program produced a synergistic two-wavelength light-dose regimen (LDR) that inactivates catheter biofilms by up to ≈7 log₁₀ and, more remarkably, converts MRSA into a stable, antibiotic-susceptible, avirulent phenotype. Three patents protect the IP, so public disclosure is safe. There is enough here for a coordinated run of eight papers spanning photobiology, microbiology/AMR, biomedical optics, genomics, and military medicine.

Hard constraint: there is no laboratory available and the strains have been destroyed — so no new wet-lab data (no new MICs, cultures, or assays). Every manuscript below is built from data we already hold, or from computational re-analysis of data we already own (the genomics paper). The full GeneWiz dataset was delivered on a hard drive, which is the only missing piece for #5.

TARGETBiofilm (Elsevier, open access) — explicitly publishes biofilm methods/models. Alternatives: npj Biofilms and Microbiomes (Article); JoVE (companion video protocol); Journal of Microbiological Methods

Example abstract

Reproducible, clinically representative biofilms are the rate-limiting reagent in evaluating device-disinfection technologies. We describe a continuous-flow biofilm reactor (“Gliese 623B”) and an end-to-end assay that grows mono- and multi-species biofilms on the luminal surface of ISO 594 Tygon 3350 catheter segments under defined hydrodynamic and nutrient conditions. Segments are conditioned with a fibrin/alginate/serum film, inoculated, and perfused (≈1 mL/min) for 48–72 h to yield mature biofilms of S. aureus, S. epidermidis, P. aeruginosa and C. albicans, alone or combined. We detail the reactor build, biosafety blow-out/dry-run testing, media, inoculation verification, and a destructive recovery assay for log-density enumeration, with SEM confirmation of architecture. The method yields 10⁶–10⁷ CFU per segment with defined precision, supporting quantitative dose-response testing.

Why it’s worthwhile

Most publication-ready item we have and needs no new data — the 55-page protocol, Runs 0–7 and SEM imagery already exist. It becomes the citable reference anchor for manuscripts #2–#4 and complements (rather than competes with) your 2018 CMR biofilm-methodology review.

TARGETJournal of Photochemistry and Photobiology B: Biology — core venue for antimicrobial photoinactivation; you have published here. Alternatives: Photochemistry and Photobiology (ASP); Photochemical & Photobiological Sciences (Springer); npj Biofilms and Microbiomes

Example abstract

Ultraviolet germicidal irradiation is dose-limited by tissue/material tolerance and microbial repair. We report that combining sub-germicidal UV-A (365 nm) under pulse-width modulation (100 Hz, 75% duty cycle) with continuous UV-C (265 nm) produces strong synergy against S. aureus biofilms inside catheter lumens. Across a designed set of >1,400 measurements analyzed by multiple-regression and principal-component analysis, the combined regimen achieved up to ≈7 log₁₀ inactivation at fluences far below either wavelength alone (≈100-fold less UV-A and 3–4-fold less UV-C), a statistically significant ≈1 log₁₀ improvement over the model intercept. Concurrent pulse-modulated UV-A without pretreatment was optimal; saline lumen filling and strain identity were significant negative covariates. We propose sub-cidal UV-A stimulates endogenous chromophores (including staphyloxanthin) that UV-C then photo-oxidizes to ROS, compounding direct UV-C DNA damage.

Why it’s worthwhile

Synergy at ≈100× lower UV-A dose is a real, quantitative photobiology result with immediate device-design relevance (lower dose = safer, faster, lower-power). The PCA/regression backbone (R. Santos) is rigor most UV-inactivation papers lack.

Fig. (generated): fluence needed for ≈6 log₁₀ reduction of a 10⁶ CFU biofilm — the LDR reaches it with ≈100× less UV-A and 3–4× less UV-C than either wavelength alone.

  • Materials & methods — emitters/wavelengths; dosimetry; biofilm model (cite #1); design matrix; statistics
  • Results — calibration — Petri-dish dose-response for UV-A and UV-C
  • Results — biofilm dose-response — catheter-segment kill across the variable matrix

TARGETnpj Antimicrobials and Resistance (Nature Portfolio, OA) — scope explicitly covers mechanisms of action/resistance and genomics of resistance. Alternatives: Antimicrobial Agents and Chemotherapy (ASM); Microbiology Spectrum (ASM); mBio; (reach: Advanced Science)

Example abstract

Antimicrobial resistance erodes the utility of established antibiotics. We report that a synergistic two-wavelength light-dose regimen (LDR; concurrent 265 nm UV-C and pulse-modulated 365 nm UV-A) not only inactivates S. aureus biofilms but selects survivors with a fundamentally altered phenotype. Survivors from treated MRSA (ATCC 33592) biofilms were depigmented, producing ≥6-fold less staphyloxanthin (below detection) and losing β-hemolysis on blood agar, consistent with attenuated virulence. By CLSI disk-diffusion criteria survivors were susceptible to all six antibiotics tested; critically, the four agents to which the parent was resistant — oxacillin (reversal of methicillin resistance), cefprozil, rifampin and tetracycline — became susceptible. The susceptible, depigmented phenotype was stable through serial passage on antibiotic-free media across two generations. Comparative transcriptomics showed marked divergence concentrated in the translational apparatus. Confirmation by broth-microdilution MIC is a noted limitation.

Why it’s worthwhile

“Using light to make MRSA treatable again” is the single most novel, compelling story in the dataset — sitting at the intersection of AMR, photobiology and virulence. It is patent-protected (disclosure is safe), and the honest framing (disk diffusion + four orthogonal phenotypes) is defensible without new data.

TARGETJournal of Biomedical Optics (SPIE) — fits optical-engineering + dosimetry + device-validation content. Alternatives: Lasers in Surgery and Medicine; American Journal of Infection Control (IPC framing); npj Biofilms and Microbiomes

Example abstract

Catheter-related bloodstream infections motivate point-of-care disinfection of the catheter–connector interface, but delivering germicidal UV around an ISO 594 luer is an unsolved optics problem. We describe a reusable, self-sterilizing fused-silica light-pipe with a machined male-luer taper that couples a multi-emitter UV-A/UV-C package into the luer and lumen while a light-blocking harness suppresses photoreactivation. Zemax ray-trace modeling and fiber-optic power mapping quantified delivery and identified dominant loss terms. Relative to a single-LED predecessor, treatable distance increased ≈10-fold and treatable area ≈270-fold, with high-log biofilm inactivation and a dedicated self-sterilization mode. We report transparently where the prototype fell short in independent testing — optical cold spots from debris on the taper, an underpowered budget (≈10% of light reaching the luer), and incompatibility with needleless access devices — and define the redesign these imply.

Why it’s worthwhile

A complete optical-engineering arc (design → modeling → bench efficacy → independent validation), patent-protected, and the candid failure-mode post-mortem is genuinely useful and rarely published. It also de-risks future commercialization/grant work by documenting exactly what redesign is needed.

Example abstract

Survivors of a two-wavelength UV light-dose regimen exhibit a stable, depigmented, antibiotic-susceptible, avirulent phenotype. To define the molecular basis we analyzed comparative RNA-seq of MRSA ATCC 33592 and two survivor lineages (triplicate design). Whereas parental reads mapped as expected to the reference, survivor reads mapped at dramatically reduced rates — a divergence so large the vendor initially suspected contamination, but which taxonomic classification confirms is genuine, heavily remodeled Staphylococcus sequence rather than foreign DNA. Differential expression and manual annotation localized the most extreme changes to the translational machinery (tRNA loci) alongside altered capsule and surface/secretion determinants, consistent with slow growth, regulatory dysfunction, and loss of staphyloxanthin and hemolysis. We re-map all libraries to the isogenic ATCC 33592 genome to provide a fair comparison and quantify true divergence.

Why it’s worthwhile

Supplies the mechanistic backbone for the flagship and publicly rebuts the contamination misread — exactly the objection a reviewer will raise. The tRNA/translation-apparatus signal is real and interesting. Re-mapping to the correct reference is the single most valuable computational fix available — and it needs no laboratory.

Fig. (generated): RNA-seq read mapping to the S. aureus reference — 78% for the parent vs ≈5% for survivors. This is genuine sequence divergence from photodamage, not contamination (confirmed by taxonomic classification).

Example abstract

As antibiotic pipelines lag resistance, light-based antimicrobials are re-emerging as complements. This review synthesizes how ultraviolet, violet-blue, and photosensitizer-mediated approaches inactivate pathogens and, increasingly, alter them — targeting protective pigments such as staphyloxanthin, exploiting endogenous chromophores to generate ROS, and selecting against virulence and resistance traits. We compare wavelengths, waveforms, and combination regimens; examine evidence that light can re-sensitize MDR organisms and attenuate virulence; and assess the translational landscape for wound, device, and environmental applications, including photoreactivation risk and the methodological standards (biofilm models, dosimetry) needed for reproducible progress.
